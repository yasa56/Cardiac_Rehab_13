What Is Cardiac Rehabilitation?

Cardiac rehabilitation (CR) is a medically supervised, multidisciplinary program of structured exercise training, cardiovascular risk factor modification, education, and psychosocial support designed to optimize the physical and psychological recovery of patients with established cardiovascular disease. CR is recognized by the AHA, ACC, and AACVPR as a Class I, Level A recommendation for most post-cardiac event patients.

CR is not merely supervised exercise — it encompasses a comprehensive continuum of care that begins during hospitalization and extends into lifelong self-management, addressing modifiable risk factors, medication adherence, dietary change, psychological wellbeing, and return to productive activity.

Core Goal To stabilize, slow, or reverse the progression of cardiovascular disease, and to reduce the risk of future cardiac events through a structured, evidence-based, multidisciplinary program.

Indications for Cardiac Rehabilitation

Class I Indications (AHA/ACC)

  • Acute myocardial infarction (STEMI/NSTEMI) — within past 12 months
  • Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG)
  • Stable angina pectoris
  • Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI/stent)
  • HFpEF)" data-def="A condition where the heart muscle is weakened and cannot pump blood efficiently enough to meet the body's needs. The heart has not stopped — it is still working — but like an engine running at reduced power, it struggles to keep up. Fluid can accumulate in the lungs and legs as a result." data-matters="Carefully supervised exercise in cardiac rehab is one of the best proven treatments for heart failure — it strengthens the heart and significantly improves how you feel every day.">Chronic heart failure (HFrEF, HFpEF) — stable
  • Cardiac valve repair or replacement
  • Heart transplantation
  • Peripheral artery disease (PAD)

Absolute Contraindications

  • Unstable angina (not yet stabilized)
  • Uncontrolled arrhythmias causing symptoms or hemodynamic compromise
  • Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis
  • Decompensated heart failure (acute)
  • Acute myocarditis or pericarditis
  • Recent pulmonary embolism or deep vein thrombosis
  • Resting SBP >180 mmHg or DBP >110 mmHg
  • Active endocarditis

The Four-Phase Framework

Phase I
Inpatient
Day 1–7 post-event. Mobilization, education, risk stratification, and discharge planning.
Phase II
Supervised Outpatient
2–12 weeks post-discharge. ECG-monitored exercise, intensive education, risk factor modification.
Phase III
Community
Community gym or home-based. Semi-supervised. Transition to independent exercise maintenance.
Phase IV
Long-Term
Lifelong. Self-managed exercise, annual reviews, secondary prevention continuation.

Patient Assessment & Initial Evaluation

Systematic intake, history-taking, functional testing, and baseline documentation

Initial CR Assessment — Core Components

Medical History

  • Primary cardiac diagnosis and event date
  • Relevant comorbidities (DM, CKD, COPD, PAD, obesity)
  • Complete surgical/procedure history (CABG, PCI, valve, ICD/PPM)
  • Family history of premature CVD (<55 men, <65 women)
  • Medication reconciliation — current list and adherence
  • Allergies and adverse medication reactions
  • Prior CR participation and outcomes

Lifestyle & Psychosocial History

  • Smoking status — current/former/never; pack-years
  • Alcohol use (AUDIT score)
  • Physical activity level pre- and post-event
  • Dietary habits — 24-hour recall, food preferences
  • Occupational demands and return-to-work goals
  • Social support systems
  • Anxiety, depression screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7)

Phase I — Inpatient Cardiac Rehabilitation

Phase I  Day 1 through hospital discharge · Duration: 3–7 days

Goals of Inpatient CR Phase I

  • Prevent deconditioning and complications of bed rest
  • Counteract psychological distress (fear, depression, denial)
  • Initiate patient and family education
  • Perform early risk stratification
  • Assess functional capacity for safe discharge
  • Plan outpatient CR referral (Phase II)
  • Ensure medication understanding and adherence
  • Address modifiable risk factors early

Phase I Education — Core Topics

Understanding the Cardiac Event
Anatomy of the heart and coronary arteries, pathophysiology of MI or the relevant event, what the procedure entailed (PCI, CABG, etc.), healing timeline and what to expect, importance of medication adherence, warning signs requiring emergency evaluation.
Medications Review
Purpose and dose of each discharge medication. Aspirin, P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor) — duration and missed-dose protocol. Beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, statins, diuretics. Common side effects and what to report. Emphasize: never stop antiplatelet therapy without physician guidance.
Activity Guidance at Discharge
Gradual return to activity — walking progression. Lifting restrictions (post-CABG: no >5–10 lbs for 6–8 weeks). Driving restrictions (jurisdiction-dependent; typically 4 weeks post-MI, 2 weeks post-PCI). Sexual activity typically safe once asymptomatic with 2 flights of stairs. No heavy exertion until CR clearance.
Dietary Guidelines (Initial)
Mediterranean-style eating. Reduce LDL targets needed to protect your arteries after a cardiac event.">saturated fat to <7% of total calories. Eliminate trans fats. Reduce dietary sodium to <2 g/day (especially HF patients). Increase omega-3 rich foods (fatty fish 2×/week). Increase fiber (30g/day). Moderate alcohol or abstain. Weight management goals if BMI >25.
Smoking Cessation
Absolute priority. Smoking cessation reduces recurrent MI risk by 50% within 1 year. Brief motivational counseling. Offer NRT (nicotine replacement therapy), varenicline, or bupropion per guidelines. Refer to smoking cessation program. Address secondhand smoke exposure. AACVPR recommends pharmacotherapy in all current smokers.
When to Call 911 / Go to ER
Call 911 immediately for: Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or heaviness lasting >5 minutes (especially at rest) · Shortness of breath at rest · Unexplained loss of consciousness or near-syncope · Rapid or irregular heartbeat with dizziness · Sudden severe headache, vision changes, or facial drooping (stroke symptoms) · New leg swelling with calf pain (DVT risk)

Phase II — Supervised Outpatient Cardiac Rehabilitation

Phase II  Weeks 1–12 post-discharge · ECG-monitored · 36 standard sessions

Phase II Structure & Operational Requirements

Program Requirements (AACVPR)

  • Medical director (physician) oversight required
  • Licensed clinical staff: exercise physiologist, RN, or PT
  • Emergency equipment: AED/defibrillators (Automated External Defibrillators) are designed for use by non-medical staff." data-matters="The defibrillator is the single most important piece of emergency equipment in a cardiac rehab facility — early defibrillation within 3–5 minutes of cardiac arrest dramatically improves survival rates.">defibrillator, crash cart, O₂
  • ECG monitoring capability (telemetry or 12-lead)
  • Written emergency response plan, practiced biannually
  • Individual exercise prescriptions for each patient
  • 36 sessions covered by Medicare (with qualifying diagnosis)

Typical Session Structure (60–90 min)

Pre-exercise Check (10 min)

Vital signs, symptoms review, ECG rhythm check, weight (HF patients), medication changes

Warm-Up (10 min)

Light aerobic activity, dynamic stretching. HR target: <50% HRR

Aerobic Training (30–45 min)

Per individualized prescription. Continuous or interval format

Resistance Training (10–15 min)

2× per week. Moderate load, high reps. Upper and lower body

Cool-Down (10 min)

Gradual intensity reduction, static stretching, flexibility work

Post-exercise Assessment (10 min)

Vital signs, symptoms, ECG, education component, session documentation

Phase III — Community-Based Cardiac Rehabilitation

Phase III  Months 3–12 · Transitional phase · Semi-supervised exercise maintenance

Phase III Goals & Transition Criteria

Readiness for Phase III Transition Completed minimum 24 supervised Phase II sessions · Demonstrated safe self-monitoring of exercise intensity · Stable functional capacity (6MWT ≥300 m or >5 METs on ETT) · No exercise-induced arrhythmias or hemodynamic instability in last 6 sessions · Patient educated in Borg scale and HR target zones · Emergency action plan understood by patient

Phase III Settings

  • Community fitness centers with CR-familiar staff
  • YMCA/YWCA Cardiac Rehab programs
  • Hospital outpatient wellness centers
  • Home-based monitored programs (remote monitoring devices, telephone or video check-ins, and digital platforms. Research shows outcomes are equivalent to supervised centre-based programs for low-to-moderate risk patients." data-matters="Home-based CR removes transport and scheduling barriers — making it possible for more patients to complete the full program and gain its life-saving benefits.">HBCR)
  • Digital/telehealth hybrid CR programs

Exercise Parameters — Phase III

  • Frequency: 3–5 days/week
  • Duration: 30–60 min continuous aerobic activity
  • Intensity: Borg RPE 12–15 or 40–70% HRR
  • Mode: Walking, cycling, swimming, elliptical
  • Resistance: 2–3×/week, 1–3 sets, 10–15 reps
  • Review appointments: Every 3 months

Phase IV — Long-Term Self-Managed Maintenance

Phase IV  Year 1 onward · Lifelong secondary prevention

Lifelong Secondary Prevention Goals

Core Message for Patients Cardiac rehabilitation is not a course to complete — it is a lifestyle to maintain. The benefits of exercise training reverse within 4–8 weeks of deconditioning. Long-term adherence is the single greatest predictor of sustained cardiovascular benefit.

Annual Review Components

  • 6-Minute Walk Test or equivalent functional assessment
  • BP, resting HR, weight, waist circumference
  • Fasting lipid panel, HbA1c, renal panel
  • Medications review and adherence
  • Reassess depression/anxiety (PHQ-9)
  • Review exercise log and barriers
  • Update exercise prescription if needed

Minimum Activity Targets (AHA 2023)

  • ≥150 min/week moderate-intensity aerobic activity
  • OR ≥75 min/week vigorous-intensity activity
  • OR equivalent combination (MET-minutes)
  • Muscle-strengthening ≥2 days/week
  • Reduce prolonged sedentary time
  • Flexibility and balance training for adults >65

Target Heart Rate Calculation Methods

Karvonen Formula (HRR Method)

HRR = HRmax – HRrest
THR = (HRR × %intensity) + HRrest

Example (moderate 50–70% HRR):
HRmax = 155 · HRrest = 65
HRR = 90 bpm
THR = 65 + (90 × 0.50) = 110
THR = 65 + (90 × 0.70) = 128
Target: 110–128 bpm
🧮 Calculate Your Personal Heart Rate Zone
How this works in three steps:
Step 1 — Your age estimates your maximum heart rate (220 − age).
Step 2 — Subtracting your resting heart rate from that maximum gives your Heart Rate Reserve (HRR) — the full range your heart can use during exercise.
Step 3 — A percentage of that reserve (based on your chosen intensity) is added back to your resting rate to give your personal target zone.
Your age is used to estimate your maximum heart rate using the formula 220 − age. For example, age 65 → max HR = 155 bpm. This is a population average estimate — your actual max may differ slightly.

The Borg Scale — Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

📖 What is the Borg Scale?
The Borg Scale is a simple way to measure how hard you feel you are working during exercise — using a number from 6 to 20. You pick the number that best matches your effort right now. There are no right or wrong answers — it is purely how you feel. The scale was designed so that multiplying your score by 10 gives a rough estimate of your heart rate (e.g., RPE 14 ≈ 140 bpm). This makes it especially useful when heart rate alone cannot be trusted — for example, when taking beta-blockers.

HIIT should be reserved for Phase II–III patients who have completed ≥12 sessions and are hemodynamically stable.

ICD/PPM Management in CR

Essential Pre-Exercise Information for Device Patients Obtain device check/clinic report before first CR session. Know: device type, lead configuration, pacing mode, lower rate limit, upper tracking rate, ICD therapy zones (VT detection rate, shock threshold). Confirm pacemaker-dependency status. Understand device-specific HR ceiling for exercise.

ICD Patients — Exercise Guidelines

  • Exercise HR must remain >20 bpm below lowest ICD detection zone
  • Example: ICD therapy threshold 150 bpm → max exercise HR 130 bpm
  • If inappropriate shocks documented: physician clearance before CR
  • Post-shock: 48–72 hour exercise hold; physician re-clearance required
  • Contact sports and water immersion: device-specific guidance required
  • Educate patient: sensing artifact from muscle movement (pectoral) can trigger inappropriate therapy

Permanent Pacemaker — Exercise Guidance

  • Rate-responsive pacemakers (VVIR, DDDR): HR will increase with exercise appropriately
  • Fixed-rate pacemakers: HR ceiling at programmed rate — use RPE primarily for intensity
  • Avoid upper extremity ipsilateral to implant for 4–6 weeks post-implant
  • No contact sports until wound fully healed and lead matured
  • MRI: confirm MRI-conditional device status before any imaging referral
  • Annual remote monitoring strongly encouraged

Emergency Protocols & Safety Management

Cardiac arrest response, acute coronary syndrome recognition, CPR for rhythm analysis, AED protocols

Cardiac Arrest Response — CR Setting

⚡ Critical: Exercise CR Cardiac Event Rate The risk of cardiac arrest during supervised Phase II CR is approximately 1 per 60,000–80,000 patient-hours. All CR staff must be BLS-certified. ACLS certification strongly recommended for supervising clinicians. AED must be accessible within 60 seconds of any exercise area.
1

Recognize Cardiac Arrest

Unresponsive, absent or abnormal breathing (gasping), absent pulse. Do NOT delay — time to defibrillation is the primary determinant of survival.

2

Activate Emergency Response

Call out to team: "We have a code!" · Assign roles: compressor, AED operator, airway manager, timer, recorder · Call 911 immediately (do not delay CPR for 911)

3

Begin High-Quality CPR

Rate 100–120/min · Depth ≥2 inches (5 cm) · Full chest recoil after each compression · Minimize interruptions <10 seconds · 30:2 ratio if no advanced airway · Rotate compressor every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue

4

Attach AED — Defibrillate ASAP

Power on AED · Apply pads (right infraclavicular, left lateral) · Stop CPR for rhythm analysis · If shock advised: announce "Clear!", ensure no contact, deliver shock · Resume CPR immediately (do not wait to check pulse)

5

Advanced Airway & ACLS

BVM ventilation if trained · Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 min (ACLS team) · Amiodarone 300 mg IV for refractory VF/pVT · Identify and treat reversible causes (H's and T's)

6

Post-ROSC Management & Documentation

Vital signs, 12-lead ECG, STEMI evaluation · Maintain SpO₂ 94–98% · Avoid hyperthermia · Complete incident report · Debrief team · Notify next of kin · Cooperate with EMS handoff

THE H's
  • Hypovolemia
  • Hypoxia
  • Hydrogen ion (acidosis)
  • Hypo/hyperkalemia
  • Hypothermia
THE T's
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Tamponade (cardiac)
  • Toxins (drug overdose)
  • Thrombosis (coronary — ACS)
  • Thrombosis (pulmonary — PE)

Acute Coronary Syndrome Recognition & Response in CR

ACS Warning — Typical and Atypical Presentations Classic: substernal chest pressure/tightness, radiation to left arm/jaw/back, diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea. Atypical (women, DM, elderly): isolated dyspnea, fatigue, epigastric discomfort, nausea/vomiting, no chest pain. Any new or different symptom in a CR patient warrants immediate evaluation.

Immediate Actions

1

Stop Exercise Immediately

Have patient sit or lie down. Do not leave unattended.

2

Vital Signs & 12-Lead ECG

Within 60 seconds. BP both arms if aortic dissection possible.

3

Nitroglycerin (if prescribed)

0.4 mg SL q5 min × 3 if SBP >90 mmHg and no PDE-5 inhibitor use in 24–48h. Call physician immediately.

4

Aspirin 325 mg (if not contraindicated)

Chewed (non-enteric coated). If patient already on antiplatelet therapy, notify physician before additional aspirin.

5

Call 911 if No Rapid Improvement

Symptoms not resolving in 15 min, ECG changes, hemodynamic instability → activate EMS immediately.

Nutrition & Dietary Counseling in CR

Evidence-based dietary patterns, cardiovascular nutrition science, and patient counseling frameworks

Dietary Patterns — Evidence Summary

Tier 1 Evidence — Mediterranean Diet

The PREDIMED trial (7,447 patients) demonstrated a 30% reduction in major cardiovascular events in high-risk patients following a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts vs. low-fat diet.

Core Components

  • Olive oil as primary fat source (≥4 tbsp/day EVOO)
  • Vegetables: ≥2 servings/day, fruits ≥3/day
  • Legumes: ≥3 servings/week
  • Fish (especially fatty): ≥3 servings/week
  • Nuts: ≥3 servings/week (unsalted, raw)
  • Whole grains over refined carbohydrates
  • Red meat: <1 serving/week
  • Moderate wine (1 glass/day if drinking — not recommended to initiate)
DASH Diet

Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension. Reduces SBP by 8–14 mmHg. Emphasizes fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains. Limits sodium to <2,300 mg/day (1,500 mg/day ideal). Especially useful in hypertensive CR patients.

Key Nutrients to Limit
  • Saturated fat: <7% of daily calories
  • Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils): eliminate entirely
  • Dietary cholesterol: <300 mg/day (<200 mg/day if high LDL)
  • Added sugar: <25 g/day (AHA)
  • Sodium: <2 g/day in HF; <2.3 g/day general CR
  • Processed and ultra-processed foods

Evidence-Based Psychological Interventions

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
CBT is the first-line psychological intervention for depression and anxiety in cardiac patients. Targets maladaptive thought patterns, catastrophizing, and avoidance behaviors. 8–12 sessions individually or in group format. Cochrane review: significant reduction in depression and anxiety scores in CAD patients.
Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)
8-week structured program. Reduces cortisol, inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), and perceived stress. Particularly effective for patients with high hostility and anger. May improve HRV (autonomic balance). Complementary to formal CR exercise program.
Cardiac Support Groups
Peer support reduces social isolation, improves self-efficacy, and adherence to CR programs. Mended Hearts (USA) and similar organizations provide patient-to-patient support. CR programs should facilitate group education sessions and encourage peer connection.
Sexual Health Counseling
Sexual activity is equivalent to 3–5 METs (walking at moderate pace). Safe for most CR patients once they can perform 3–4 METs without symptoms. Address medication effects (beta-blockers: PDE-5 inhibitors: contraindicated with nitrates). Proactively open the topic — patients rarely ask but universally need guidance.

Special Populations in Cardiac Rehabilitation

Heart failure, post-CABG, post-valve surgery, LVAD, elderly, women, and diabetes

Heart Failure (HFrEF & HFpEF)

HF-Specific Assessment Before Each CR Session Weight (compare to dry weight baseline) · Bilateral ankle edema assessment · JVP estimation · Lung bases auscultation · Note orthopnea or PND since last session · Current diuretic dose changes

Weight-Based Exercise Hold Rule (HF)

  • Weight gain >1–2 kg overnight: Withhold exercise; notify physician
  • Acute dyspnea at rest: No exercise; urgent evaluation
  • New or worsened peripheral edema: Reassess before exercise
  • SpO₂ <90% at rest: No exercise; oxygen therapy; physician contact
  • HR <50 or >100 at rest: Physician review before session

HF Exercise Modification

  • Start at very low intensity (40–50% HRR, RPE 11–12)
  • Shorter sessions (10–20 min) initially, build to 30–45 min
  • Interval training often better tolerated than continuous
  • Resistance training: moderate loads (40–50% 1-RM), higher reps
  • Avoid Valsalva maneuver — risk of hemodynamic compromise
  • Inspiratory muscle training (IMT) if inspiratory weakness present

Post-CABG Cardiac Rehabilitation

Sternal Precautions (Standard)

  • No lifting >5–10 lbs for 6–8 weeks
  • Avoid pushing/pulling with arms (up from chair: use legs)
  • No driving for 4–6 weeks (varies by jurisdiction)
  • Sternal wound: monitor for erythema, dehiscence, drainage
  • Upper extremity ROM: progress gradually; avoid end-range initially
  • Bilateral sternal stabilization when coughing/sneezing (pillow)

Post-CABG Exercise Notes

  • Radial artery harvest: wrist flexion/extension exercises PRN
  • Saphenous vein harvest: leg edema common; compression stockings, limb elevation
  • New conduction abnormalities (LBBB, AF) common early post-CABG — monitor carefully
  • Pericardial effusion: be alert for new dyspnea, tamponade signs
  • Post-pericardiotomy syndrome (2–4 weeks): fever, pleuritic chest pain, friction rub — temporary hold; physician evaluation

LVAD (Left Ventricular Assist Device) in CR

LVAD Safety — Non-Negotiable Requirements LVAD coordinator must be consulted before any CR enrollment. Driveline exit site must remain dry — no swimming or water immersion. Controller and batteries must be fully charged and secure. Map (Mean Arterial Pressure) monitoring — not HR — is primary hemodynamic indicator in most LVAD patients (pump provides continuous flow).

Hemodynamic Monitoring — LVAD

  • BP by Doppler (standard cuffs unreliable in CF-LVADs)
  • MAP target: 70–90 mmHg during exercise
  • LVAD pump parameters: speed (RPM), flow (L/min), power (watts), pulsatility index
  • SpO₂ continuous monitoring
  • RPE as primary intensity guide

Exercise Prescription — LVAD

  • Begin after driveline site healing (typically 4–6 weeks post-implant)
  • Start: 10–15 min walking, RPE 11–13
  • Avoid Valsalva, isometric exercises (ventricular suction risk)
  • Resistance training: light loads initially, physician-guided progression
  • Contact sports: absolutely contraindicated
  • Aquatic therapy: contraindicated (driveline moisture risk)

Elderly Patients (≥75 years) in CR

Geriatric Assessment Add-ons

  • Clinical Frailty Scale" data-def="Frailty is a clinical syndrome of reduced physiological reserve — marked by weakness, slow walking speed, low physical activity, exhaustion, and unintentional weight loss. The Fried Phenotype identifies frailty by scoring these five criteria. The Clinical Frailty Scale rates function on a 1–9 scale from very fit to terminally ill." data-matters="Identifying frailty early allows your rehab team to design a gentler, function-focused program — frail patients benefit enormously from rehabilitation but need a modified approach to avoid injury and optimize gains.">Frailty screening: Fried Phenotype or Clinical Frailty Scale
  • Cognitive screening: MoCA or MMSE (medication adherence, safety)
  • Gait speed / Timed Up-and-Go (TUG) test
  • Fall risk assessment — particularly on beta-blockers and diuretics
  • Polypharmacy review (>5 medications = increased adverse event risk)
  • Social supports, caregiver availability, transport barriers

Exercise Adaptations — Elderly

  • Prioritize functional fitness (ADL performance) alongside aerobic fitness
  • Balance and fall prevention exercises: mandatory
  • Resistance training: especially important (sarcopenia counteraction)
  • Flexibility and joint ROM: extended warm-up and cool-down
  • Reduce exercise intensity during heat or humidity
  • Longer recovery periods between sessions
  • Group format beneficial for adherence and socialization

Women in Cardiac Rehabilitation — Unique Considerations

Underrepresentation Gap Women represent only 30–35% of Phase II CR participants despite making up 40–45% of those with CAD. Barriers include referral bias, caregiver responsibilities, transportation, and atypical symptom presentation. Active outreach and flexible scheduling improve female participation rates.

Physiological Differences

  • Lower aerobic capacity at baseline vs. age-matched men
  • More likely to present with microvascular angina (non-obstructive CAD)
  • Post-menopausal accelerated cardiovascular risk
  • Higher rates of anxiety and depression post-cardiac event
  • Greater risk of statin myopathy (pharmacogenomic differences)

CR Adaptations for Women

  • Flexible scheduling (caregiver role accommodation)
  • Women-only group sessions if available and preferred
  • More intensive psychosocial screening and support
  • Address fear of exercise — common barrier in women
  • Monitor for atypical anginal equivalents (fatigue, jaw pain, nausea)
  • Include bone health (osteoporosis) in lifestyle counseling

Documentation Requirements — Per Session

Pre-Exercise Documentation

  • Date, session number, patient identifier
  • Pre-exercise HR, BP, weight (HF patients), SpO₂
  • Symptoms since last session
  • Medication changes (new, stopped, dose changes)
  • Rhythm strip or ECG (per monitoring protocol)
  • Any contraindications or modifications noted

Exercise & Post-Exercise Documentation

  • Exercise modalities used, duration, HR, RPE
  • Resistance training: exercises, sets, reps, load
  • Symptoms during exercise (angina, dyspnea, arrhythmia)
  • Peak HR, peak BP, recovery HR at 1 and 5 minutes
  • Post-exercise SpO₂, resting HR, BP
  • Education topic covered this session
  • Clinician signature and credentials
Electronic Health Record (EHR) Integration CR sessions should be documented in the patient's primary EHR for continuity of care. Key data points for cardiologist co-management: exercise tolerance trend, arrhythmia events, weight trend (HF), risk factor status, medication adherence issues, psychosocial concerns, and attendance rate.
